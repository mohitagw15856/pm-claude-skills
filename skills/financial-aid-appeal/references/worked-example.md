# Worked Example — Financial Aid Appeal

A gold-standard run of this skill: a documented job-loss appeal with the gap computed, the ask specific, and exactly one human sentence.

## The brief

Daniel Okafor is a rising sophomore at Hartwell College (a fictional private school), aid package $31,000/year against a $58,000 cost of attendance. In March his mother — the household's primary earner — was laid off from her hospital-administration job; household income drops from $92,000 to $54,000 (her severance ends in June; his father's income continues). The family contribution was set at $18,000; they can now realistically manage $9,000. Daniel has: the termination letter dated March 14, the final severance statement, and last year's aid award letter. The appeal window closes June 30; it's June 3. He drafted a two-page letter that opens with three paragraphs about his mother's dedication to her career and asks the office to "please consider any additional support possible."

## The output

### Financial Aid Appeal: Daniel Okafor, ID H-221478 — Hartwell College, Fall 2026

June 3, 2026
Office of Financial Aid, Hartwell College

Dear Financial Aid Committee,

I am writing to appeal my 2026–27 aid package based on a change in my family's financial circumstances since my original application. On March 14, 2026, my mother was laid off from her position at Riverside Health System, where she was our household's primary earner; her severance ends June 30 (termination letter and final severance statement attached).

Our household income has fallen from $92,000 (as reported on my original application) to a projected $54,000 for 2026–27. The effect on our family contribution:

| | Original application | Current |
|---|---|---|
| Household income | $92,000 | $54,000 (projected) |
| Family contribution | $18,000 | $9,000 (realistic maximum) |
| Resulting gap | — | **$9,000** |

I am requesting an additional **$9,000 in grant aid** for the 2026–27 year to close this gap. If grant funds cannot cover the full amount, I would welcome a conversation about a combination of grant aid and work-study.

Hartwell is where I want to finish what I've started — my sophomore research placement in the psychology department is already arranged, and I intend to graduate here. Thank you for considering this appeal; I am glad to provide any further documentation the committee needs.

Sincerely,
Daniel Okafor

**Enclosures:** (1) Termination letter, Riverside Health System, dated March 14, 2026 · (2) Final severance statement · (3) 2026–27 aid award letter · (4) Parent statement of projected 2026–27 income

### Evidence checklist
| Claim | Document | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Layoff occurred, and when | Termination letter, 3/14 | in hand |
| Income now vs then | Severance statement + parent projected-income statement | severance in hand; **parent statement: one page, signed — write it this week** |
| Original numbers | Last year's award letter | in hand |
| Contribution can be $9,000 | Implied by income documents — no separate doc needed; do not invent one | — |

### Follow-up plan
- **June 6 (day 3):** call to confirm receipt: "I submitted an appeal on June 3 and want to confirm it's complete — is anything missing from my file?"
- **June 13 (day 10):** polite status check, same phrasing, ask for expected decision timing against the June 30 window.
- **June 20:** if no decision timeline, ask whether an aid officer can review the file before the window closes, and whether Daniel's parents joining a call would help.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The change and its date open the letter** — "laid off on March 14, primary earner, severance ends June 30" is in sentence one, because appeals succeed on changed circumstances and the officer decides in the first paragraph whether this file has one.
- **The three paragraphs about his mother's career are gone** — the framework's rule is numbers-then-feelings; the termination letter carries the story better than the tribute did.
- **The ask is $9,000 in grant aid, not "any additional support possible"** — offices can approve a number; the draft's vague ask was a request for the minimum. The grant-vs-loan distinction is stated because it matters.
- **Exactly one human sentence survives** — the research-placement line, which doubles as the commitment-to-this-school signal the framework calls for.
- **The table computes the gap instead of gesturing at it** — then/now pairs for every number, each traceable to an enclosure.
- **The checklist caught a missing document** — the projected-income parent statement — before the office would have caught it; an incomplete file is the slowest possible "no."
- **Follow-up dates are real calendar dates against the real June 30 window** — the deadline drives the plan, per the miss-the-window anti-pattern; the letter went out good-today rather than perfect-in-July.
