# Worked Example — Moving Company Estimate Decoder

A gold-standard run of this skill: a suspiciously cheap long-distance quote decoded into two walk-away flags and one fixable one — before the truck had the furniture.

## The brief

Sana is moving a 2-bedroom apartment ~1,900 km for a new job. "Statewide Relocation Group" quoted **$2,850** — notably under two other quotes ($4,300 and $4,700) — based on a 10-minute phone call, no survey. The emailed agreement shows: "**non-binding estimate**, final charges based on actual **cubic feet**" · deposit: **$900 due at booking** · delivery: "7–21 business days" · valuation: "$0.60 per pound per article included free" · the confirmation email footer names a different company ("SRG Logistics LLC") · payment: "balance due in cash or money order prior to unloading." She's excited about the price and wants to book today.

## The output

### Moving Estimate Decode: 2BR, ~1,900 km — "Statewide Relocation Group"

**1. The verdict** — **Don't book this one.** This estimate combines four of the pattern's five classic flags: non-binding + cubic-feet pricing on a long-distance move, a large upfront deposit, no survey behind a firm-sounding number, and a second company name in the paperwork. The $1,450 "savings" vs. your other quotes is the setup, not the deal — the reweigh/re-cube at pickup is where the number grows, enforced at delivery when they have your things and the contract says cash before unloading.

**2. The scenarios** — Quoted: $2,850 · Likely: **$4,800–6,200** ("your shipment measured larger than estimated" at pickup — cubic feet are unauditable by design, and non-binding means the new number governs) · Worst-case: the hostage pattern — goods on the truck, revised bill at destination, "storage fees accrue until balance is paid in cash."

**3. Decode table**

| Term | What the document says | What it means at delivery | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Non-binding estimate" | Final = actual charges | The quote is marketing; the pickup-day number is the price | 🔴 |
| Cubic-feet pricing | "based on cubic feet" | Volume is re-measurable and inflatable; weight at certified scales is auditable — this choice is informative | 🔴 |
| $900 deposit at booking | Upfront | Reputable movers typically collect at delivery; big deposits fund the pattern | 🔴 |
| Two company names | Quote vs. email footer | A broker handoff — you don't know who shows up, and accountability diffuses | 🔴 |
| "Cash or money order prior to unloading" | Payment terms | The enforcement mechanism for the revised number | 🔴 |
| "7–21 business days" delivery | A window, not a date | A month without your things is inside the contract | 🟡 |
| $0.60/lb valuation | "Included free" | See the valuation math — this is not insurance | 🟡 |

**4. The valuation decision** — $0.60/lb on *her* goods: the 12-lb laptop = **$7.20**; the 90-lb sofa = $54; the 6-lb camera bag with $3,000 of gear = **$3.60**. Released value protects almost nothing she cares about — with any legitimate mover, price full-value protection as the real insurance decision.

**5. Verification checklist + questions** — (for any candidate, including the two pricier quotes): interstate/long-distance registration lookup with the national regulator (type varies by country — verify), the same-name check across quote/contract/truck, an in-home or video survey before any binding number, **binding-not-to-exceed** in writing with a total, deposit small-or-none, accessorials (stairs, long-carry, shuttle) priced now, recent reviews under the *exact legal name* of both names seen.

**6. The redirect** — Take the $4,300 quote through the same checklist: surveyed? binding-not-to-exceed available? payment at delivery? If yes ×3, the $4,300 was the cheap quote all along.

*"This is a plain-language reading, not legal/financial advice — laws vary by jurisdiction; confirm anything load-bearing with a qualified professional."*

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The estimate type led the verdict** — non-binding on long-distance is the framework's first check because everything else (the reweigh, the cash-at-delivery clause) operationalizes it; the type names the bill's real author.
- **The scenarios show the mechanism, not just the number** — "measured larger at pickup" is *how* $2,850 becomes $5,500; per the quality checks, mechanism beats arithmetic for changing a decision made in excitement.
- **The two-company-names detail got full weight** — the broker handoff is the pattern's quietest tell and the accountability sink; the same-name check runs even when nothing else looks wrong.
- **The valuation math used her actual items** — $3.60 for the camera bag is the released-value reality; computed on real goods per the framework, because the abstract version ("$0.60/lb") sounds like coverage.
- **The honest majority stayed honest** — the decode condemns a pattern, not an industry, and its constructive ending re-routes her to the legitimately-priced quote with the checklist that verifies it; the walk-away and the redirect are the same skill working.
- **Booking-today urgency was answered with process** — the checklist is deliberately doable this week; the counter to "the price expires" is never speed, per the family's anti-patterns.
