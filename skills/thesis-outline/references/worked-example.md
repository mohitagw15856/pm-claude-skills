# Worked Example — Thesis Outline

A gold-standard run of this skill: a sociology master's thesis outlined argument-first, with an overloaded chapter caught by evidence budgeting and the weakest link named before the committee names it.

## The brief

Lena is eight months from submitting a sociology MA thesis on gig-economy couriers and algorithmic management. Research question: *how do food-delivery couriers exercise agency under algorithmic management?* Draft answer: they develop shared workaround practices — but these mostly optimize *within* the platform's rules rather than resisting them. What exists: 22 semi-structured courier interviews (transcribed), 6 months of field notes from courier meetup groups, a literature-review draft whose gap statement is "resistance studies focus on formal workplaces; algorithmic-management studies focus on the algorithm, not the worker." Program wants 5–7 chapters, ~35,000 words. Her supervisor is on record that "everything interesting is in the interviews" and is skeptical of grand resistance framing. Lena's current outline: Introduction / Literature Review / Methodology / Findings / Discussion / Conclusion — with "Findings" holding everything.

## The output

### Thesis Outline: "Working the algorithm: courier agency under algorithmic management"
**Thesis sentence:** Food-delivery couriers exercise agency chiefly through shared *optimization practices* that master the algorithm's rules rather than resist them — a form of agency existing frameworks misread because they equate agency with resistance.
**The argument in four moves:** 1) Existing frameworks offer only compliance-or-resistance. 2) Couriers demonstrably act with agency (the practices). 3) But the practices optimize within the rules (the twist). 4) So the compliance/resistance binary needs a third term — *mastery* — with consequences for how we theorize algorithmic control.

#### Chapter map
| Ch | Title | Job in the argument | Central claim | Evidence carried |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Introduction | Stake the question and the twist | The binary fails a growing class of workers | vignette from field notes |
| 2 | Resistance and its absences | Establish the gap (move 1) | The literature can't see non-resistant agency | lit review (gap statement) |
| 3 | Methodology | License the claims | 22 interviews + 6-month ethnography can support practice-level claims (not prevalence claims) | design, access, positionality |
| 4 | The practices | Move 2 — show agency | Couriers share deliberate, learned practices (zone timing, order-stacking, rating repair) | interviews (primary), field notes |
| 5 | Mastery, not resistance | Move 3 — the twist | The practices optimize within platform rules; couriers describe pride, not sabotage | interviews re-read against ch. 2's frameworks |
| 6 | The third term | Move 4 — the contribution | "Algorithmic mastery" as a category, its scope and limits | synthesis; field notes on practice-sharing |
| 7 | Conclusion | Land it | What mastery means for platform-labor debates | — |

#### Handoffs
Ch1→2: "Seeing why this puzzle is invisible requires examining what the literature equips us to see." Ch2→3: "Having located the gap, the question is what evidence could fill it." Ch3→4: "The methods established, the practices themselves come into view." Ch4→5: "These practices prove agency — but agency of what kind?" Ch5→6: "If neither compliance nor resistance fits, the frameworks need a third term." Ch6→7: "Named and bounded, mastery's implications extend beyond couriers."

#### Examiner's risk register
| Weak link | Where | The probe they'd make | Shoring-up plan |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mastery/resistance boundary | Ch 5–6 | "Isn't mastery just resistance you've renamed? Rating-repair *games* the system." | Define the boundary by couriers' own framing + whether the practice violates platform rules; concede the borderline cases explicitly in ch. 6 |
| Generalizability from 22 interviews | Ch 3 | "One city, one platform — why should we believe 'couriers' rather than 'these couriers'?" | Claim practice-existence, never prevalence; say so in ch. 3 and again in ch. 7's limits |
| The lit review's second front | Ch 2 | "You engage resistance studies but algorithmic-management theory only thinly." | Two-sided gap: add the Kellogg/Valentine/Christin-style control-typology strand to ch. 2 — it's also ch. 5's toolkit |
| Field notes underused | Ch 4, 6 | "You had 6 months of ethnography — where is it?" | Field notes own the *sharing/transmission* story (how practices spread at meetups) — that's ch. 6's empirical spine, not decoration |

#### Cut-or-justify
- A planned section on gig-economy regulation/policy — **cut**: serves no move in the four-move argument; one paragraph in ch. 7 at most.
- Courier demographics analysis — **justify or shrink**: keep only what ch. 3 needs to license the claims; it's a methods table, not a chapter section.
- The "history of algorithmic management" 4,000 words drafted for ch. 2 — **shrink to 1,000**: ch. 2 earns its place by establishing the gap, nothing more.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The thesis fits in one sentence with its twist intact** — mastery-not-resistance — and the four moves are the sentence unpacked; per the framework, chapters exist to carry those moves, so the outline was rebuilt from the answer backwards.
- **"Findings" was split into chapters 4 and 5** — the original container chapter hid the argument's best move (show agency, *then* complicate it); now each half has a job and a claim, and the twist gets its own chapter instead of a subsection.
- **Every chapter row names its claim** — "Ch 4: couriers share deliberate, learned practices" is arguable and evidenced; "Chapter 4: Findings" was a container, which the framework calls the first way a thesis dies.
- **Evidence budgeting caught the starving asset** — the six months of field notes were feeding nothing while the interviews fed everything; the fix (field notes own the practice-*transmission* story in ch. 6) turned a committee vulnerability into the contribution chapter's spine.
- **The handoffs are written now, in the outline** — the through-line test made inspectable; if a handoff can't be written, the chapter order is wrong and it's cheaper to learn that here.
- **The risk register leads with the actual weakest link** — the mastery/resistance boundary is the thesis's load-bearing distinction, so it's the first thing a skeptical examiner probes; the shoring plan concedes the borderline cases rather than hiding them, per the don't-hide-the-weakest-link anti-pattern.
- **Cut-or-justify shrinks the outline to the finishable thesis** — the regulation section died and the history section shrank because neither serves the thesis sentence, and eight months is a budget, not a suggestion. The supervisor's known views ("it's all in the interviews," resistance-skeptic) are worked *with*: the outline centers the interviews and frames the contribution as a correction to resistance framing, not a defense of it.
