# Worked Example — Literature Review Builder

A gold-standard run of this skill: nine sources on remote work and productivity, organized into debates instead of a paper parade, with a gap that actually follows from the review.

## The brief

Sofia, an honors-thesis student in organizational psychology, has read nine sources on remote work and knowledge-worker productivity and pasted her notes: Bloom et al. 2015 (RCT at a Chinese travel firm, call-center workers, +13% performance working from home); Choudhury et al. 2021 (patent examiners, work-from-anywhere, +4.4% output); Gibbs et al. 2023 (IT-services firm, hours up, output flat → productivity down 8–19%); Yang et al. 2022 (61k Microsoft employees, network analysis, collaboration became more siloed); Allen et al. 2015 (review, moderators matter — task interdependence especially); Bailenson 2021 (theory, "Zoom fatigue" mechanisms); Barrero, Bloom & Davis 2023 (survey program, hybrid preferences and the amenity value of WFH); Emanuel & Harrington 2024 (call-center natural experiment, remote hires' productivity lower but selection effects large); Aksoy et al. 2022 (cross-country survey, commute-time savings). Her research question: *does hybrid work improve productivity for interdependent teams?* Style: APA. She's drafted one paragraph that goes "Bloom found… Choudhury found… Gibbs found…"

## The output

### Literature Review Skeleton: Remote work and knowledge-worker productivity
**Research question:** Does hybrid work improve productivity for teams with high task interdependence?
**The gap (draft):** The field measures individuals and networks separately; almost nothing measures *interdependent teams under hybrid schedules* — the modal arrangement since 2022.

#### Theme 1: Does remote work raise or lower individual output? (The measurement fight)
The headline disagreement — Bloom et al. (2015) and Choudhury et al. (2021) find gains (+13%, +4.4%) where Gibbs et al. (2023) find an 8–19% productivity *decline* — dissolves partly on method: the gain studies measure countable output (calls handled, patents examined) in independent-task jobs, while Gibbs et al. measure output *per hour worked* in interdependent IT work, where hours quietly expanded. Emanuel and Harrington (2024) complicate both directions: remote workers' lower productivity in their data is substantially a *selection* story — who chooses remote — not a treatment effect. The theme's honest summary: the sign of the effect tracks task type and denominator choice more than it tracks "remote."

#### Theme 2: The collaboration cost — individual gains, network losses
Yang et al. (2022) shift the unit of analysis: individual output can hold while the *network* silos — fewer bridging ties, more static collaboration. Bailenson (2021) supplies a mechanism at the meeting level (videoconference fatigue), and Allen et al.'s (2015) moderator framework predicts exactly this split: costs concentrate where task interdependence is high. This theme reframes Theme 1's fight — the studies finding gains measured jobs where the network barely matters.

#### Theme 3: What workers will trade for it
Barrero, Bloom and Davis (2023) and Aksoy et al. (2022) establish WFH as an amenity — workers value it at ~8% of pay, and commute savings are real. This is not a productivity claim, but it bounds the managerial question: hybrid may be worth adopting at productivity-neutral even before any gain.

#### The Gap
As reviewed, the field offers individual-level RCTs in independent-task jobs (Theme 1), network-level observational evidence of siloing (Theme 2), and preference evidence (Theme 3) — but no study measures *team-level output for interdependent teams under structured hybrid schedules*, which is precisely where Theme 1's gains and Theme 2's costs collide. This study addresses that intersection.

#### Unread Register — NOT YET READ
- Battiston, Blanes i Vidal & Kirchmaier (face-to-face communication and productivity) — cited by Yang et al. and Gibbs et al.; likely the closest existing work to the gap. Chase first.
- Bloom, Han & Liang 2024 (hybrid RCT at Trip.com) — cited by Barrero et al.; a hybrid RCT would directly pressure the gap statement. **Read before defending the gap.**
- Brucks & Levav 2022 (virtual meetings and idea generation) — cited by Bailenson; relevant to Theme 2's mechanism.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **Sections are debates, not papers** — Theme 1 is a fight ("raise or lower?"), not a topic ("productivity studies"); the skeleton replaced Sofia's "Bloom found… Choudhury found…" paragraph, which the anti-patterns call a list wearing paragraphs.
- **Every paragraph holds ≥2 sources in relation** — Gibbs *contradicts* Bloom, Emanuel-Harrington *complicates both*, Yang *reframes* the whole theme — the conversation move, applied throughout.
- **Conflicting findings are resolved through methods first** — the Bloom-vs-Gibbs contradiction is examined as countable-output-vs-output-per-hour and independent-vs-interdependent tasks, per framework rule 3, instead of being flattened into "scholars have various views."
- **The funnel is visible**: broad field (Theme 1) → the specific tension (Theme 2's unit-of-analysis shift) → the unanswered intersection (the Gap). The gap *follows from* the reviewed material — Themes 1 and 2 jointly construct it.
- **Only provided sources appear in the text** — Battiston et al. and the Trip.com RCT clearly matter, but Sofia hasn't read them, so they live in the unread register marked NOT YET READ rather than in the review as if read; the register even flags which one could break her gap statement.
- **The skeleton stops at scaffolding** — sources placed, tensions named, handoffs implied — but the honors-thesis argument in Sofia's voice is left to Sofia, per the thesis-level anti-pattern.
