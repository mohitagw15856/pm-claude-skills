# Evidence, Not Adjectives — calibration for review language that survives scrutiny

Reviews fail in two directions: inflation (everyone "exceeds expectations" until the layoff list makes a liar of the file) and vagueness (adjectives that can't be acted on or defended). Both are fixed by the same move: every claim gets an observable.

## The substitution table
| Adjective (delete) | Evidence (write instead) |
|---|---|
| "great communicator" | "her incident summaries were forwarded by two other teams as the template" |
| "needs to be more proactive" | "in 3 of 4 launches, dependencies he owned surfaced in the final week" (and: was surfacing them ever made safe?) |
| "strong technical skills" | "reduced the pipeline's p95 from 40 to 9 minutes; wrote the runbook others debug with" |
| "not a team player" | banned until translated — it usually means either "disagreed with me" (not a flaw) or a specific behaviour (name it) |
| "exceeds expectations" | exceeded *which* expectation, set *when*, in *what artifact*? If goals were never written, the rating system is fiction — fix that first. |

## Calibration rules that keep the file honest
- **The surprise test:** nothing in a review may be news. If it is, the failure is the manager's cadence, and the review must say so rather than ambush.
- **The evidence ratio:** one written observable per claim, minimum. A review with 10 claims and 2 observables is 80% mood.
- **Recency bias check:** pull one example per quarter, not three from the last month. Calendars and shipped-work lists beat memory.
- **The peer-quote trap:** anonymous peer feedback quoted verbatim is an accountability laundry. Synthesise patterns ("three collaborators independently raised handoff clarity") — patterns are defensible; quotes are shrapnel.
- **Trajectory over snapshot:** the most useful sentence in any review names the delta ("six months ago this needed my review; now I sign without reading") — it's also the sentence people remember.

## For the difficult review
Write the PIP-honest version first, then soften the *tone*, never the *facts*. A kind review that hides a performance problem is the cruellest document in the file: it forfeits the person's chance to fix it and the company's ability to act.
