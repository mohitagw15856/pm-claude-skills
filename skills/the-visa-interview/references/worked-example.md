# Worked Example — The Visa Interview

A gold-standard run of this skill: a student-visa applicant with a truthful but badly-shaped story — over-rehearsed where it should be plain, rambling where it should be short — reshaped without a word of it being invented.

## The brief

Arjun, 23, has an admit for an MS in data science abroad. The true situation: father's textile business funds year one (~$60k liquid shown), an education loan covers year two; his uncle lives in the destination country (listed on the form); he honestly hopes to work a few years there after graduating under post-study work rules, then return to the family business as it digitizes. His prepared answers are memorized paragraphs; when nervous, he over-explains. His dread: the uncle question — a cousin told him to "just not mention" the uncle. The form already lists the uncle. Visa type: student; country simulated generically, verify-officially flagged.

## The output

# Visa Interview: student — [generic consular window]

> Simulation — a plausible adversarial reading, not a prediction.

## Transcript (excerpted)

**Officer:** "Why this university?"
**Arjun (memorized):** "This esteemed institution's world-class faculty and cutting-edge curriculum align perfectly with my academic aspirations and—"
*Officer's read:* Coached paragraph; tells me nothing. Follow-up triggered.
**Officer:** "Which course are you most looking forward to?"
**Arjun (off-script, better):** "The applied ML practicum — they run it with real transit data from the city."
*Officer's read:* Specific, spontaneous, real. The plan exists. — *The debrief note: the follow-up answer should have been the first answer.*

**Officer:** "Who's funding your studies?"
**Arjun:** "My father's business — he manufactures textile trims, about forty employees. Year one is from savings, here's the statement; year two is a sanctioned education loan, letter's in the file."
*Officer's read:* Concrete, documented, scaled. Strong.

**Officer:** "Do you have relatives in [country]?"
**Arjun (the dread, played straight):** "Yes — my uncle, in [city]. He's listed on my form. He's not funding anything — my father is."
*Officer's read:* Matches the form. Volunteered the funding distinction unprompted — slightly defensive but consistent. Fine. *(The simulation's counterfactual, stated in the debrief: denying the uncle the form lists is the single fastest route to a refusal that never gets repaired — the cousin's advice was a landmine.)*

**Officer:** "What will you do after the degree?"
**Arjun (rambling):** "So my real dream is to eventually take over our family business, but also the post-study work options interest me, and honestly the industry there is amazing, my friend got a job at—"
*Officer's read:* Three answers braided; the friend detail opens a new thread I didn't ask about.
*The debrief shape (same truth, shaped):* "The program allows post-study work and I'd value a few years of industry experience if I qualify. Long-term, my father's business is digitizing and that's the role I'm building toward." — both true things, in order, and it stops.

## The Decision
Approved in this run — funding documentation and the recovered specificity carried it. The refusal-shaped version of the same interview: the uncle denial (fatal), or the memorized paragraphs holding through three follow-ups (reads as a story built for the window).

## Debrief — out of character

| Answer | Helped / hurt | The shape that works (same truth, better presented) |
|---|---|---|
| Memorized opener | Hurt | The practicum answer *was* the answer — lead with the specific, retire the paragraph |
| Funding | Helped | Keep exactly: numbers, source, documents |
| The uncle | Helped (by being truthful) | Consistency with the form is the entire game; the cousin's advice would have ended it |
| Post-study ramble | Hurt mildly | Two sentences, both true, full stop — volunteered threads invite questions |

**The consistency check:** every answer matched the form — the one place this application was already strong.
**The brevity edit:** every answer over three sentences got shorter in the debrief; none got less true.

> Prepared truthfully — this simulation improves how a real situation is presented; it does not build stories. Requirements vary by country and change: verify everything with official sources; complicated histories belong with an immigration lawyer.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The truth was never touched — only the shape** — the integrity line is structural: Arjun's real uncle, real funding, and real work hopes all survive; what changed is order, specificity, and length.
- **The cousin's concealment advice was defused in the debrief, not followed in the transcript** — the skill's hardest rule: a request to hide a material fact ends the exercise; here it's converted into the teaching moment about form consistency.
- **Over-rehearsal was punished realistically** — polish reads as coaching and triggers follow-ups, per the framework; the officer's reads show the memorized paragraph *creating* the harder interview.
- **Every officer's read names the question-behind-the-question** — "why this university" tests plan-realness, not enthusiasm — per the framework's first rule.
- **The post-study answer models honest dual intent handled plainly** — two true sentences in the right order, with country-specific rules kept generic and verify-officially flagged.
- **The refusal counterfactual is stated, once** — showing the approved run *and* naming what would have sunk it teaches more than either alone.
