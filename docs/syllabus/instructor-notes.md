# Instructor notes — running the 6 weeks

**Week 1 (Briefs):** The failure mode is students letting the AI interview *itself*. Grade the interview transcript for who steered: passing work shows the student answering hard trade-off questions, not nodding. Watch for briefs with no "out of scope" line — that's the tell of a skipped playback.

**Week 2 (Evidence):** The Tower is theatrical on purpose — the learning is in the *repair*. Require the before/after: same document, assumptions converted to sourced claims or explicitly-priced bets. A student who argues their tower SHOULD fall (the honest "this is early-stage") deserves the top grade; teach that.

**Week 3 (Exec comm):** Most students over-prepare content and under-prepare for interruption. The Defense's value is the follow-up questions; grade resilience (did answers stay short under pressure?) over polish. Common tell: reading the doc aloud when challenged = didn't internalize it.

**Week 4 (Negotiation):** 24/40 is deliberately reachable; the grade is the floor, the debrief is the lesson. Have students exchange transcripts and identify each other's un-asked questions — peers spot "you never asked why they need to close this week" faster than instructors.

**Week 5 (Reading people):** The Panel's bluffer wins against students who interview to *confirm*. The teachable moment is the second follow-up ("walk me through the number") — collect the class's wrong calls and read the tells they missed aloud, anonymized.

**Week 6 (Calibration + Exam):** Predictions logged in week 6 pay off after the course — schedule a 30-day reunion to settle them; that session teaches more about calibration than the course did. The Exam is open-book by design: it tests judgment, not recall, and the credential's verifiability is the anti-cheat.

**Grading load:** ~10 min/student/week — every artifact is either attested (verify in seconds) or a one-page read. That's the design.
